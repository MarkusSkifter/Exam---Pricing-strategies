\qhead{2. Making Pricing Decisions, Value and Prices — What are your product's reference and differential values? What are your price floor and price ceiling? Explain your answer.}

For the Qin Plus DM-i I apply the exchange-value framework, \(EV = RV + DV\) \parencite{nagle2018}.

\textbf{Reference value (RV).} The next-best alternative for a mass-market Chinese buyer is not another EV but the \textbf{petrol car} they would otherwise buy — a VW Lavida or Toyota Corolla at roughly RMB~100{,}000--130{,}000. That anchors the reference value.

\textbf{Differentiation value (DV).} \emph{Monetary:} fuel savings of up to about RMB~9{,}682 a year, the new-energy-vehicle tax exemption, and lower running cost. \emph{Psychological:} modern technology, free ``God's Eye'' driver assistance, and environmental status. Both are positive, so \(EV\) sits well above the petrol reference.

\textbf{Price floor.} The relevant floor is BYD's \textbf{incremental cost}, not overhead. Vertical integration and the cell-to-pack Blade Battery — roughly \euro10/kWh cheaper than nickel-cobalt cells \parencite{motleyfool2025} — push this floor unusually low.

\textbf{Price ceiling.} The ceiling is exchange value: the petrol reference price plus monetised differentiation. Above it, consumer surplus turns negative and the buyer reverts to petrol.

\textbf{The decision.} BYD prices the Qin \emph{below} the petrol reference while delivering \emph{higher} value, leaving large consumer surplus and converting ICE buyers. The key nuance: BYD is \textbf{cost-advantaged but value-led} — low cost is the enabler, but the logic of the price is exchange value versus the reference car, not cost-plus.

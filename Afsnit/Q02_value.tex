\qhead{2. Making Pricing Decisions, Value and Prices. What are your product's reference and differential values? What are your price floor and price ceiling? Explain your answer.}

To judge what the Qin Plus DM-i is worth I use the exchange-value idea, where the most a buyer will rationally pay is the reference value plus the differentiation value \cite{nagle2018}, and the two together fix a range of admissible prices.

The reference value is the price of whatever the customer would otherwise buy, and for an ordinary Chinese family that is a petrol car, a Volkswagen Lavida or a Toyota Corolla at roughly \euro15{,}000. On top of that sits the differentiation value. The monetary part is money the owner keeps: a fuel saving of around \euro1{,}240 a year, worth close to \euro10{,}000 over eight years of ownership, plus the exemption from the new-energy-vehicle tax and lower maintenance, perhaps a few thousand more. The psychological part, harder to price but real, covers the modern technology, the free driver-assistance system, and the sense of doing something for the environment.

Putting rough numbers on the limits, the ceiling is the exchange value, the \euro15{,}000 reference plus those savings, which lands in the low-to-mid \euro20{,}000s. The floor is not full accounting cost but the extra cost of building one more car, and the Blade Battery, about ten euros per kilowatt-hour cheaper than the older nickel-cobalt cells \cite{motleyfool2025}, plausibly holds it under \euro10{,}000. The admissible range is the gap between the two, and the space the buyer keeps inside it is the consumer surplus.

What BYD does is set the sticker near the bottom of that range, below the petrol car it is measured against, while giving more value, which is exactly what ``electricity cheaper than oil'' claims. The point worth making is that this is not cost-driven pricing in disguise: the low cost lets BYD survive there, but the price is set against the petrol alternative. The same logic lifts the ceiling in Europe, where the reference cars are dearer and the buyers wealthier.

\qhead{9. The Impact of Competition Upon Prices — What structural conditions most affect your pricing? How?}

The Chinese EV market is a \textbf{repeated prisoner's dilemma}: each firm gains share by cutting price, but if all cut, every firm lands in the mutual-loss cell of falling industry profit \parencite{nagle2018}. BYD's 19\% profit fall is that cell \parencite{byd2025}.

The structural conditions that most affect BYD's pricing are precisely those that, in the framework, \textbf{block} the tacit collusion that would otherwise hold prices up:

\begin{itemize}[nosep,leftmargin=1.3em]
  \item \textbf{Many competitors} (100+ NEV brands): coordination is hard and defection hard to monitor.
  \item \textbf{Asymmetric costs}: BYD's cost edge means it does \emph{not} share rivals' interest in high prices — it profits from a low-price equilibrium that bankrupts them, breaking the ``commonality of interests'' collusion needs.
  \item \textbf{Overcapacity and slowing growth}: pressure to cut prices to fill plants.
  \item \textbf{Transparent prices}: cuts are detected instantly, triggering fast retaliation.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{How this shapes BYD — the asymmetric-cost twist.} Tacit collusion needs \emph{similar} firms. Because BYD is the low-cost outlier, \textbf{defection is its dominant long-run strategy}: its retaliation is cheaper to sustain than rivals'. That is why no stable equilibrium forms, the price war persists, and ultimately the \emph{government} — not the rivals — had to intervene where tacit collusion could not.

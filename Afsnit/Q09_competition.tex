\qhead{9. The Impact of Competition Upon Prices. What structural conditions most affect your pricing? How?}

The Chinese electric-car market behaves like a repeated prisoner's dilemma. Any one firm can win share by cutting its price, but when everyone cuts, they all end up worse off as industry profit falls, and BYD's 19 percent profit drop is exactly that outcome \parencite{byd2025, nagle2018}. In a game that repeats, firms can in principle hold prices up through tacit collusion, but only when the structure of the market supports it, and here most of the conditions point the other way.

Several features matter. With more than a hundred brands in the market, coordination is almost impossible and a quiet price cut is hard to spot. There is heavy overcapacity and growth is slowing, so everyone feels pressure to cut just to keep the factories full. Prices are very transparent, which means any cut is noticed at once and answered quickly. And most importantly the firms are not alike: BYD's cost advantage means it does not share its rivals' interest in keeping prices high, because it actually profits from a low-price world that drains the others, and that breaks the common interest tacit collusion depends on.

This last point is what really shapes BYD's pricing. Tacit collusion needs firms that look roughly the same and break even at roughly the same price. BYD is the odd one out on cost, so the usual logic inverts. It can hold a low-price position that hurts its rivals more than itself, which makes a cut from BYD credible and effectively permanent, and that is why no stable, cooperative price level ever forms. The telling sign is that the discipline the rivals could not impose on each other has had to come from the government instead, through its campaign against destructive over-competition and its move to reform the Pricing Law. So competition shapes BYD's prices less through what any rival charges and more through the simple fact that its cost asymmetry puts the cooperative outcome out of reach.

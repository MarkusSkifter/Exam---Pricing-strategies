\qhead{10. Price Leadership and Price Wars — Is there a price leader in your industry? How does it perform its role? Are the others followers or challengers? Do you see potential for a price war?}

\textbf{Is BYD a price leader?} Partly. Good pricing conduct needs \emph{visibility}, \emph{common motivation} and \emph{resolve} \parencite{nagle2018}. BYD has visibility (the largest, most-watched player) and resolve (it cuts credibly and sustains), but lacks \textbf{common motivation}: its cost edge means it \emph{wants} lower prices than rivals. So instead of leading the industry \emph{up} — the stabilising role of a true price leader — it leads it \emph{down}, acting as a price-war \textbf{instigator} (cuts above 30\% on some models in May 2025 \parencite{restofworldWar}). Rivals are reluctant followers rather than challengers, because matching BYD's prices erodes their thinner margins faster than BYD's.

\textbf{Potential for a war?} It is already underway, and the risk checklist is high on nearly every factor: stagnant growth, capacity glut, fixed-cost-heavy production, low differentiation in mass EVs, price-driven buyers, transparent prices, and strong retaliation ability.

\textbf{Should a rival match?} The response flowchart for a higher-cost legacy OEM gives a clear answer: the cut is real and broad; large volume is at risk; BYD's objective is share plus driving weak rivals out; but the OEM \emph{cannot} sustain the war (higher break-even, Question~7). \textbf{Conclusion: do not match on price.} The correct moves are non-price — reinforce differentiation, retreat to a defensible premium niche, or exit the price-sensitive segment, since against a lower-cost attacker the higher-cost firm loses first.

\textbf{Should BYD have started it?} The ``justified'' conditions largely fit — a sustainable cost advantage, a price-sensitive market, and the ability to outlast rivals. Yet ``there are no winners in a price war'' holds even here: BYD's own profit fell 19\% and regulators intervened \parencite{byd2025}, which is why its smartest exit is to migrate toward value-based, differentiated pricing.
